What Is the Software Development Life Cycle?
The software development life cycle is a process used within a software organization to complete a software project.
It is a thorough strategy that describes how to build, maintain, replace, and change or improve certain software.
The life cycle defines an approach for improving software quality and the development process as a whole.
The following figure is a representation of the various stages of a typical SDLC.
1. Planning and Requirement Analysis
2. Defining Requirements
3. Designing the Product Architecture
4. Building or Developing the Product
5. Testing the Product
6. Deployment in the Market and Maintenance

Characteristics of the Maintenance Phase
Matt is a project manager tasked with delivering software to an insurance company. The product is deployed
to the insurance firm after Matt's project team completes the coding and implementation phases of the software
development life cycle (SDLC). Now that the insurance business has the software and it is functioning well,
Matt's team meets with the insurance firm to deliver instructions for the software's maintenance and upkeep.

What Is Improper Error Handling?

___________________________________________________________________________

___________________________________________________________________________
Improper error handling happens when errors are handled incorrectly, resulting in errors being presented to
the end user, which creates unpleasant responses from the user and opens the door for exploits to be
developed to capitalize on these weaknesses. This is why great efforts are taken during an application's
development cycle to ensure that no information about the application's processing is exposed during
The fundamental benefit of blacklisting is its ease of use. The system can be configured to exclude recognized
risks, and the intelligence is held by the software vendor. Its efficacy is entirely dependent on how frequently
the vendor distributes or updates the known threat database. All updates are fetched automatically from the
user's perspective. Users have no way of distinguishing between good and harmful data transmission unless
the software says so. The main disadvantage of blacklisting architecture is the inability to cope with unknown
threats. For example, ransomware continues to evolve, with previously unseen patterns and varieties.
